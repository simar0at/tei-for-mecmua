Encoding

Only the Epistle to the Hebrews is covered here.

This is a digital rendition of Sanders' 1918 edition that applies methods described in the article "Manuscript Markup" by Timothy J. Finney. Whereas in most respects it is a faithful reproduction of Sanders' edition, there are some differences:

  • The manuscript's bar-like diacritical marks are converted to the appropriate breathings or diaeresis.
  • The arrangement and extent of supplements are sometimes changed; iota subscripts, diacritics and punctuation within supplements are omitted. Dots in a supplement are replaced by a gap element that indicates the approximate number of letters not supplied. The number is estimated by presuming a line length of 24 letters.
  • Quire numbers are placed immediately before pages that contain them.
  • At page 111, line 3, κατευπασεν is corrected to κατεπαυσεν.
  • The transcription of page 113, lines 6-8, is revised.
  • η̣βουληθησα̣ν̣ (p. 125, line 6) is rewritten as η̣βουληθης α̣ν̣.

Three referencing systems are employed, using milestones to mark state transitions: (1) book, chapter and verse; (2) quire, page and line; (3) paragraphs. The third system records scribal paragraph divisions.

Notes

*1. Sanders [1918, 296] has κατευπασεν by mistake.

*2. Sanders [1918, 297] transcribes p. 113, lines 6-8, as:
[προς τον δυ]ν̣α̣μενον σωζειν α̣υ̣τ̣ο̣(ν)
[εκ θανατου, μετα] κ̣ρ̣αυγης εισχυ
[ρας και δακρυων προ]σ̣ε̣ν̣ε̣[γκας,]
and notes [296] that αυτον is perhaps written in the margin by the second hand. A narrow-band image of this page shows no sign of αυτον in the margin. The same image indicates that lines 6 and 7 end with σωζειν and κραυγη̣[ς], respectively. Such an arrangement would place αυτον at the beginning of line 7.

*3. This may be βαπτισθενν. [Sanders 1918, 297]

*4. The first hand wrote ου in an erasure. [Sanders 1918, 298]

*5. Sanders [1918, 301] has η̣βουληθησα̣ν̣, which does not make sense in the context. The reading does make sense if η̣βουληθης and α̣ν̣ are separated: You expressed a wish, above, saying that you neither desired nor took pleasure in sacrifices and offerings... (I owe this insight to Dr. Richard K. Moore, my PhD supervisor.)
